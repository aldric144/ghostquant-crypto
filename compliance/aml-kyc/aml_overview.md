GhostQuant™ is a cryptocurrency intelligence platform that provides advanced analytics, behavioral profiling, and risk detection capabilities to financial institutions, law enforcement agencies, and regulatory bodies. As a provider of financial intelligence services that support anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) operations, GhostQuant™ must maintain comprehensive compliance with global AML regulations.

This document establishes GhostQuant™'s commitment to AML/KYC compliance and demonstrates how our intelligence engines support regulatory obligations under the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) regulations, Financial Action Task Force (FATF) recommendations, EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD5/6), and Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) sanctions programs.

---



**Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) - 31 USC 5311 et seq.**

The BSA requires financial institutions to assist U.S. government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering. Key requirements include:
- Customer Identification Program (CIP)
- Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
- Suspicious Activity Reporting (SAR)
- Currency Transaction Reporting (CTR)
- Record retention (5 years minimum)

**FinCEN Regulations - 31 CFR Chapter X**

FinCEN regulations implement the BSA and establish specific requirements for:
- Know Your Customer (KYC) procedures
- Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for high-risk customers
- Beneficial ownership identification
- Suspicious activity monitoring and reporting
- Information sharing among financial institutions

**USA PATRIOT Act - Title III**

The USA PATRIOT Act strengthened AML requirements by:
- Expanding the definition of financial institutions
- Requiring enhanced due diligence for foreign correspondent accounts
- Prohibiting shell banks
- Requiring special measures for jurisdictions of primary money laundering concern
- Establishing information sharing provisions

**OFAC Sanctions Programs - 31 CFR Chapter V**

OFAC administers and enforces economic sanctions programs targeting:
- Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List
- Sectoral Sanctions Identifications (SSI) List
- Foreign Sanctions Evaders (FSE) List
- Non-SDN entities
- Country-based sanctions programs

---


**Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Recommendations**

FATF is the global standard-setter for AML/CFT. The 40 Recommendations cover:
- Risk assessment and mitigation
- Customer due diligence and record-keeping
- Reporting of suspicious transactions
- Regulation and supervision
- International cooperation
- Virtual asset service providers (VASPs)

**EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives**

**AMLD5 (Fifth Anti-Money Laundering Directive):**
- Extended AML requirements to virtual currency exchanges and wallet providers
- Enhanced beneficial ownership transparency
- Strengthened customer due diligence requirements
- Expanded PEP definitions
- Lowered thresholds for customer identification

**AMLD6 (Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive):**
- Harmonized definition of money laundering offenses across EU
- Extended criminal liability to legal persons
- Increased maximum prison sentences (minimum 4 years)
- Expanded list of predicate offenses
- Enhanced cross-border cooperation

**EU Travel Rule (Regulation 2023/1113):**
- Requires transfer of originator and beneficiary information for crypto transfers
- Applies to transfers above €1,000 (or equivalent)
- Requires verification of beneficiary information
- Establishes record-keeping requirements

---


**UK Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (as amended)**
- Implements FATF recommendations and EU directives
- Requires risk-based approach to AML/CFT
- Establishes registration requirements for crypto businesses
- Requires enhanced due diligence for high-risk situations

**Singapore Payment Services Act 2019**
- Licenses digital payment token service providers
- Requires AML/CFT compliance programs
- Establishes customer due diligence requirements
- Requires transaction monitoring and suspicious transaction reporting

**Hong Kong Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing Ordinance**
- Extends AML/CFT requirements to virtual asset service providers
- Requires licensing by Securities and Futures Commission
- Establishes customer due diligence and record-keeping requirements
- Requires suspicious transaction reporting

While GhostQuant™ is not a traditional financial institution, virtual asset service provider (VASP), or money services business (MSB), we provide intelligence services that directly support AML/CFT compliance operations. Our regulatory obligations arise from:

1. **Service Provider to Regulated Entities:** GhostQuant™ provides intelligence services to banks, exchanges, and other financial institutions that are subject to AML regulations. As a critical service provider, we must maintain compliance standards commensurate with our clients' obligations.

2. **Data Processing and Analysis:** GhostQuant™ processes transaction data, identity information, and behavioral patterns that are used for AML/CFT purposes. We must ensure data integrity, confidentiality, and appropriate use.

3. **Sanctions Screening Support:** GhostQuant™ assists clients in identifying potential sanctions violations and high-risk entities. We must maintain accurate screening capabilities and avoid facilitating sanctions evasion.

4. **Suspicious Activity Detection:** GhostQuant™'s intelligence engines detect patterns indicative of money laundering, terrorist financing, and other financial crimes. We must ensure detection accuracy and appropriate escalation.

5. **Federal Agency Partnerships:** GhostQuant™ provides intelligence services to federal law enforcement and regulatory agencies. We must maintain compliance standards appropriate for government partnerships.

**Structuring and Smurfing:**
- Multiple transactions just below reporting thresholds
- Coordinated deposits across multiple accounts
- Rapid succession of small transactions
- Pattern of transactions designed to avoid detection

**Layering:**
- Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts
- Complex web of transactions with no apparent business purpose
- Use of multiple intermediaries
- Conversion between multiple cryptocurrencies or fiat currencies

**Integration:**
- Large purchases of legitimate assets with illicit funds
- Investment in businesses or real estate
- Repayment of loans or debts
- Charitable donations or political contributions

**Velocity Anomalies:**
- Sudden increase in transaction volume
- Unusual transaction frequency
- Rapid fund movements inconsistent with customer profile
- Burst patterns followed by dormancy
